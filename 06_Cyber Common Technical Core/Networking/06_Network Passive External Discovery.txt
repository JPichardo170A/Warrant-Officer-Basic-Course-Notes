-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Reconnaissance Stages
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Passive External – Gathering information from public sources without interacting with the target system.  
Stages    |                     Tools/Methods: WHOIS, DIG, Google Dorking, SHODAN, PDF metadata, Social Tactics.  
          |                     Targets: IP addresses, subdomains, technologies, people, historical content.  

          | Active External – Direct interaction with external-facing systems to probe for data.  
          |                     Tools/Methods: NMAP, Netcat, Banner Grabbing, PING, Curl/Wget, /dev/tcp.  
          |                     Targets: Open ports, services, banners, network reachability.  

          | Passive Internal – Monitoring internal network traffic and system data without injecting packets.  
          |                     Tools/Methods: Netstat/SS, ARP cache, Routing Table, Packet Sniffing, System DNS.  
          |                     Targets: Local processes, IP configurations, service files, OS location.  

          | Active Internal – Actively probing internal systems for vulnerabilities or misconfigurations.  
          |                     Tools/Methods: NMAP, Netcat, PING, /dev/tcp, Banner Grabbing.  
          |                     Targets: Internal ports, services, network topology, system responses.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Blue / Gray / Red Space – Cyber Operations Framework
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Blue Space – The defender’s environment; includes internal networks, systems, and users.  
Spaces    |               Example: Corporate infrastructure, user endpoints, internal security tools.  
          |               Role: Initiates actions, defends assets, monitors internal activity.  

          | Gray Space – The intermediary zone; includes public infrastructure like the Internet, DNS, and external proxies.  
          |               Example: Cloud services, public DNS servers, VPNs, third-party platforms.  
          |               Role: Acts as a bridge between Blue and Red spaces; often used for staging or obfuscation.  

          | Red Space – The adversary’s environment; includes target systems, internal proxies, and assets.  
          |               Example: Internal servers, databases, restricted networks, compromised endpoints.  
          |               Role: Final destination for offensive operations; where exploitation and pivoting occur.  

Flow of Action:
- Blue initiates reconnaissance or attack.
- Gray facilitates indirect access (e.g., via proxies or DNS).
- Red is where the target resides and actions are executed (e.g., pivoting, data exfiltration).

Template Use Case:
- A penetration tester in Blue Space uses an external proxy in Gray Space to reach internal proxies in Red Space, then pivots to internal assets for deeper access.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Reconnaissance Steps – Methodical Approach
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Network Footprinting – Initial phase focused on gathering publicly available information about the target.  
Steps     |                        Targets: Network architecture, systems in use, organizational structure.  
          |                        Methods: OSINT, WHOIS, DNS records, social media, company websites.  

          | Network Scanning – Identifying live hosts, open ports, and services running on target systems.  
          |                        Techniques:  
          |                        - Port Scanning: Detect open/closed ports (e.g., using NMAP).  
          |                        - Network Scanning: Discover active devices and IP ranges.  
          |                        - Vulnerability Scanning: Identify known flaws in services or configurations.  

          | Network Enumeration – Extracting detailed information from discovered systems.  
          |                        Targets:  
          |                        - Network resources and shared folders  
          |                        - Users and groups  
          |                        - Routing tables  
          |                        - Auditing and service settings  
          |                        - Machine names and banners  
          |                        - SNMP and DNS details  
          |                        - Other common services and ports  
          |                        Tools: SNMPwalk, Netstat, enum4linux, rpcinfo, nslookup  

          | Vulnerability Assessment – Evaluating systems for exploitable weaknesses.  
          |                        Categories:  
          |                        - Injection (e.g., SQL, command injection)  
          |                        - Broken Authentication  
          |                        - Sensitive Data Exposure  
          |                        - XML External Entities (XXE)  
          |                        - Broken Access Control  
          |                        - Security Misconfiguration  
          |                        - Software/Components with Known Vulnerabilities  
          |                        Tools: Nessus, OpenVAS, Burp Suite, Nikto

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Sock Puppet Creation – OSINT Persona Building
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Step 1: Define Persona – Choose a realistic identity (e.g., profession, age, location) that fits your investigative goals.  
Steps     |           Example: A mid-level IT consultant from Denver with interests in cybersecurity and gaming.  

          | Step 2: Generate Identity – Use tools to create a believable profile.  
          |           - Fake Name Generator – Produces full identities including name, birthday, address, and job.  
          |           - This Person Does Not Exist – AI-generated profile pictures for realism.  
          |           - ElfQrin – Alternative identity generator with deeper customization.  

          | Step 3: Build Infrastructure – Set up the digital footprint.  
          |           - Email: Use ProtonMail or Fastmail for anonymous, secure email.  
          |           - Phone: Use Mint Mobile SIM or Silent Link for temporary SMS verification.  
          |           - VPN: Set location to match persona’s geography.  

          | Step 4: Create Accounts – Register on platforms relevant to your investigation.  
          |           - Social Media: Twitter, LinkedIn, GitHub, Reddit.  
          |           - Forums: Niche communities tied to your target’s interests.  
          |           - Cloud Services: Google Drive, Dropbox (if needed for interaction).  

          | Step 5: Populate Profile – Add minimal but realistic content.  
          |           - Bio, profile picture, interests, and a few posts or likes.  
          |           - Use AI-generated home interiors (e.g., This Rental Does Not Exist) for added realism.  

          | Step 6: Maintain Anonymity –  
          |           - Avoid linking to real accounts or IPs.  
          |           - Use clean browsers, disable autofill, and clear cookies.  
          |           - Rotate VPNs and logins to simulate natural behavior.  

          | Step 7: Stay Active – Log in periodically, engage lightly, and avoid suspicious inactivity.  
          |           - Like posts, follow relevant accounts, and occasionally comment.

Template Use Case:  
- Investigating a target in the cybersecurity field:  
  - Persona: Junior analyst from Austin, TX  
  - Tools: Fake Name Generator, This Person Does Not Exist, ProtonMail  
  - Platforms: LinkedIn, GitHub, Twitter  
  - Goal: Join relevant groups, observe interactions, and collect passive intel

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Identifying Vulnerabilities
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Known Technologies – Cross-reference discovered technologies with CVE databases to identify known exploits.  
Vulns     |                        Example: Apache Struts, outdated WordPress plugins, or legacy VPN software.  
          | Error Message Responses – Analyze verbose error outputs for stack traces, misconfigurations, or exposed paths.  
          |                        Example: “SQL syntax error” or “file not found” revealing backend structure.  
          | Identify Running Services – Use tools like Nmap, Netcat, or banner grabbing to detect active services and versions.  
          |                        Example: Open ports running outdated SSH or exposed FTP servers.  
          | Identify Running OS – Use passive fingerprinting (p0f) or TTL analysis to determine operating system.  
          |                        Example: Windows systems often respond with TTL=128, Linux with TTL=64.  
          | Monitor Running Applications – Observe application behavior and exposure using Shodan, Netcraft, or passive DNS.  
          |                        Example: Detecting outdated CMS platforms, exposed admin panels, or unpatched web apps.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: DNS Intelligence Tools – WHOIS, DIG, Zone Transfer, Netcraft
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | WHOIS – Queries DNS registrar over TCP port 43 to retrieve domain registration details.  
WHOIS     |         Reveals domain owner, registrar, contact info, and creation/expiration dates.  
          |         Example: `whois zonetransfer.me`  

          | DIG – Queries DNS servers over UDP port 53 to resolve domain names and retrieve DNS records.  
DIG       |         Common Queries:  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me A` – IPv4 address  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me AAAA` – IPv6 address  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me MX` – Mail servers  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me TXT` – Text records  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me NS` – Name servers  
          |         - `dig zonetransfer.me SOA` – Start of Authority record  

          | Zone Transfer – Transfers full DNS zone data between primary and secondary servers over TCP port 53.  
Zone      |         Used for replication but can leak sensitive data if misconfigured.  
Transfer  |         Example: `dig axfr @nsztm1.digi.ninja zonetransfer.me`  
          |         Reference: [https://digi.ninja/projects/zonetransferme.php](https://digi.ninja/projects/zonetransferme.php)  

          | Netcraft – Web-based tool similar to WHOIS; provides hosting history, technologies, and server fingerprinting.  
Netcraft  |         Useful for identifying infrastructure and service providers.  
          |         Website: [https://sitereport.netcraft.com](https://sitereport.netcraft.com)

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Dig vs WHOIS – DNS Query Comparison
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | WHOIS – Queries DNS registrar over TCP port 43.  
Tool      |         Provides domain registration details including owner, registrar, and contact info.  
Comparison|         Used for administrative and ownership data.  

          | DIG – Queries DNS servers over UDP port 53.  
          |         Retrieves DNS records such as A, MX, NS, TXT, and CNAME.  
          |         Used for resolving domain names and infrastructure mapping.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                             Title: Passive OS Fingerprinter (p0f)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | p0f – Tool for passive OS fingerprinting using live traffic or packet captures.  
p0f       |         Does not send packets; analyzes existing traffic to infer OS and client/server roles.  
          |         Can guess operating system version and network stack behavior.  
          |         Useful for stealth reconnaissance and profiling targets.  
          |         Fingerprint File: `more /etc/p0f/p0f.fp`  
          |         Live Interface Scan: `sudo p0f -i eth0`  
          |         PCAP File Analysis: `sudo p0f -r test.pcap`

                                             Title: Social Tactics – Human-Centric Reconnaissance
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

          | Social Engineering – Psychological manipulation to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information.  
Human     |                        Targets emotions like trust, fear, urgency, and curiosity.  
Tactics   |                        Example: Impersonating IT support to gain login credentials.  

          | Technical-Based Attacks – Use digital channels to deliver deceptive payloads or extract data.  
Technical |                        - Email: Phishing, spoofing, malicious attachments.  
          |                        - SMS: Smishing attacks with fake alerts or links.  
          |                        - Bluetooth: Bluejacking or exploiting insecure pairing.  

          | Physical-Based Attacks – Exploit physical proximity or access to gather intel.  
Physical  |                        - Dumpster Diving: Retrieving sensitive documents from trash.  
          |                        - Shoulder Surfing: Observing screens or keystrokes in public spaces.  
          |                        - Tailgating: Following authorized personnel into restricted areas.
